Mentally and Physically Disabled

Poles

Reasons for Persecution

The Nazis considered Poles to be an inferior race and sought to reduce them to a leaderless labour class.

History

In September 1939, the German sterrorized the Polish nation in order to destory its culture and reudce the Poles to a subservient race. Thousands of Polish nobility, clergy and intellectuals were all shot, removing the "leadership class" and rendering the Poles incapable of resisting German rule.

Hitler also plannedto "Germanize" Poland by replacing the Poles with Germans. In 1942-43, approximately 100,000 Polish civilians (30,000 children) were removed from Poland.

Although the Nazis were keen to get rid of the Poles, the Nazis still needed them for labour. As a result, Police would grav Poles off streets, trains, and other public areas in order to fill quotas.

In August 1944, an underground Polish government led by Wladyslaw Sikorski coordinated a violent uprising against the Germans in Warsaw that lasted two months. Eventually though, they were crushed by the Germans and more than 200,000 Poles had been killed.

Statistics

Between 1939 and 1945 at least 1.5 million Polish citizenswere deported to Germany as free labour.

It is currently estimated that the Germans killed between 1.8 and 1.9 million non-Jewish Polish citizens and 3 million Jewish Polish citizens.

Soviet Soldiers

Reasons for Persecution

Not only did the Nazis see Soviet POWs as racially inferior, but also as potential obstacles in the German quest for lebensraum. The Nazi regime claimed that they were not obligated to care for any Soviet prisoners of war because the Soviet Union had not ratified the 1929 Geneva Convention of Prisoners of War or specifically declared its commitment to the 1907 Hague Convention on the Rules of War. Despite its truth, they were still obligated by the general international law of war to protect prisoners.

History

In August 1941, the German army set a ration of 2,200 calories per day for working Soviet POWs, though the reality was that they only received around 700 calories a day. Within a few weeks, those on the "subsistence" program had starved to death.

The Germans put very little effort into providing shelter for their Soviet POWs. Despite establishment of makeshift camps later on, the prisoners still oftentimes dug holes in the ground as shelters. By the end of 1941, many epidemics rose to the top as the main cause of death. In October 1941, almost 5,000 Soviet POWs died everyday.

Many of the prisoners captured in 1941 had to march across hundreds of miles and some were shot to death when too exhausted to continue.

German forces oftentimes shot severely wounded Soviet soldiers to rid themselves of a potential resource expenditures. Anyone who was caught trying to esacpe was shot immediately. Additionally, a decree saying that the use of arms against Soviet POWs was legal on September 8, 1941 was a clear invitation to kill as many Soviet POWs as they wanted.

In the middle of July 1941, General Hermann Reinecke allowed security forces under the Reich Security Main Office to screen Soviet POWs for politically and racially intolerable elements. Those who did show signs of intolerable elements were transferred to the SS and killed. These executions often took place in concentration camps. For example, the SS killed 65,000+ Soviet POWs over a period of six months by feeding them grass and water. In Flossenburg, the SS burned Soviet POWs to death. In Majdanek, Soviets were shot in trenches. In Mauthausen, the blood from shot POWs contaminated the local water supply.

A few camps (Auschwitz-Brikenau and Majdanek) were originally made for Soviet POWs, but January 1942 saw only a few hundred out of 10,000 Soviet POWs arrive alive. In the following, Himmler decided to fill the camps with 150,000 Jews. It was also at this time that Rudolf Hoess refined their method of execution by hydrogen cyanide.

Statistics

According to army reports, anywhere between 25% to 70% of Soviet soldiers died during the transit from occupied countries to Germany.

The number of official executions in concentration camps ranges from 140,000 to 500,000.

By February 1942, 2,000,000 out of 3,300,000 Soviet POWs in Germany had died from starvation, exposure to the elements, disease, or shooting.

Jehovah's Witnesses

Reasons for Persecution

The Nazis intensely persecuted Jehovah's Witnesses because the Witnesses would not accept the authority of the state. This unqillingness to conform came from their international connections, their strong opposition to war on behalf of a non-religious government, and strong opposition to the interference of organized governments in anything relating to one's conscience. Specifically, this appeared as a refusal to give the Nazi salute, join party organizations/let their children join the Hitler Youth, participate in elections, and hang Nazi flags.

The Nazi suspicion of Jehovah's Witnesses grew to the point where Gestapo agents infiltrated Bible study meetings, a special unit of the Gestapo was dedicated to compiling a registry of all Jehovah's Witnesses, and attempting to ban the distribution of their printed materials.

History

Very soon after the Nazi takeover, many regional governments began to aggressively push against Jehovah's Witness by breaking up their meetings and ransacking then occupying their local offices. On April 1, 1935, the Reich ordered local officials to dissolve the Watchtower Society (organization led by Jehovah's Witnesses)

When Germany reinstated compulsory military service in March 1935, conflict with the Witnesses escalated due to the illegal refusal to be drafted or perform military work. Increasingly, more and more Jehovah's Witnesses were arrested, tried, then sent to prisons and concentration camps. Despite being sent to concentration camps by the thousands and singled out with purple triangular patches, Jehovah's Witnesses continued to meet, pray, and recruit.

Despite the harsh conditions of Nazi concentration camps, Jehovah's Witnesses were sustained by a strong network of support among each other and the belief that their suffering was for God and managed to maintain their refusal to carry out military-like activities such as roll coll or rolling bandages for soldiers. At the same time, Nazi officers trusted and often used the Witnesses as servants because they refused to escape or resort to physically attacking their guards.

Statistics

By 1939, there were approximately 6,000 Witnesses detained in prisons or camps. The rest either fled Germany, continued their religious observance secretively, or left the religion altogether.

In 1933, there were 25,000 to 30,000 German Jehovah's Witnesses. Of them, an estimated 20,000 stayed active throughout the whole period. About half of the active Witnesses were at one time convicted and sentenced. At least 3,000 Jehovah's Witnesses were sentenced to concentration camps, including 200-250 Dutch, 200 Austrians, 100 Poles, and between 10-50 Belgians, French, Czechs, and Hungarians.

Of all the prosecuted Jehovah's Witnesses, approximately 1,000 German Jehovah's Witnesses, 90 Austrians, 120 Dutch Witnesses, and 190 Witnesses from other countries died during their time in camps and prisons. Additionally, there were at least 273 executions concerning Jehovah's Witnesses.

Homosexuals

Reasons for Persecution

Homosexual men were considered a danger to Nazi policies meant to raise German birthrates. Due to that reason, lesbians and non-German homosexuals were usually not targeted for persecution. In fact, Nazi leaders were quite willing to accept non-German or female homosexuals into their community so long as they controlled their "deviant" behaviour and conformed to racial ideals.

History

During the Weimar Republic (1918-1933), German homosexual-rights activists fluorished as they had never done before. When the Nazi party took over, however, they regarded the past toleration of homosexuality as a sign of decadence and a corrupting influence on German values and culture. Very early on, the Nazi party began a campaign to eliminate homosexuality that was directed by the Schutzstaffel (SS) leader, Heinrich Himmler.

On May 6, 1933, students led by the SA (Sturmabteilung/Storm Troopers) ransacted the Institute for Sexual Science in Berlin and confiscated its library. Within four days, most of the collection of over 12,000 books and 35,000 irreplaceable pictures was destroyed in the book burning in Berlin's city center. This was the first of many steps towards completely wiping out an openly homosexual culture from Germany. Police closed bars and clubs that catered specifically towards homosexuals and publications. The primary goal during this stage was to destroy all networks of support for homosexuals.

In 1934, the Gestapo ordered local police forces to maintain lists of all men engaging in gay activities. As it turned out, many of these local police offices had laready been keeping track of gay men for many years. With the rapid influx of data, Nazi officials hunted down individual homosexuals during police actions.

On June 28, 1935, the Ministry of Justice revised Paragraph 175 (section of the German Criminal Code that made illegal homosexuality between males) to provide a legal basis for extending Nazi persecution of homosexuals and expanded the category of criminally indecent acts to anything that appeared gay. Later, it was ruled that the intention or contemplation of commiting an act of homosexuality fit under the earlier requisites for arrest.

On October 26, 1936, Heinrich Himmler formed a sub-branch of the Security Police called the Reich Central Office for Combating Homosexuality and Abortion. Josef Meisinger was the director of the new office. This office held powers to preemptively arrest anyone deemed dangerous to Germany's moral fiber. Oftentimes, the police would re-arrest or send those who were recently released from prison to concentration camps

On April 4, 1938, the Gestapo announced that men convicted of homosexuality could be sent to concentration camps immediately after fulfilling their sentences. The practice of sending gay men to concentration camps wasn't new, however, as this was already being done immediately after the Nazi's seizure of power in January 1933. As a tangential point, Nazi officials often miscategorized political prisoners as homosexuals and were marked by pink triangles. According to firsthand accounts, homosexuals faced the worst treatment in the camps

Some Nazi officials designed policies to "cure" homosexuals of their disease" through hard work and humiliation. Guards often isolated homosexual prisoners and would beat them on arrival. One of these "cures" included castration. In the early stages of castration as a cure for homosexuality, gay defendants could agree to be castrated in return for lower sentences. Later on, judges and SS officials would be given the power to order the castration of any gay prisoner without their consent. Nazis were also very interested in medical experimentation during this time, and so allowed homosexual inmates to be experimented on. These "scientific" endeavours did not yield any useful results.

Statistics

It is estimated that 100,000 men were arrested as homosexuals between the years 1933 and 1945. Of these arrests, approximately 50,000 men were arrested under Paragraph 175 and 5000 to 15000 of those prisoners were sent to concentration camps. Although there are no known statistics for the death rate of homosexuals in German concentration camps, experts place it at 65%.
